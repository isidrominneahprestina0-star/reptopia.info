Overview

Green anacondas (Eunectes murinus) are among the world’s heaviest and longest snakes, reaching over 30 feet (9.1 m), 12 inches (30.5 cm) thick, and more than 550 lbs (250 kg), with females significantly larger than males. Dark olive-brown with black back spots and yellow-centered side ovals, their eyes and nostrils sit atop broad heads for seeing and breathing while submerged. These non-venomous constrictors dominate as apex predators in South America’s lush wetlands, using their immense strength to overpower prey in ambush attacks.

Habitat

Green anacondas thrive in the warm, humid lowlands of tropical rainforest, particularly the vast Amazon and Orinoco River basins spanning Brazil, Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia, Guyana, Suriname, French Guiana, and Paraguay. They favor shallow, slow-moving freshwater habitats such as swamps, marshes, flooded savannas, streams, and river edges, where dense vegetation offers cover. During seasonal floods, they roam widely, but in dry periods, they cluster near permanent water sources like deep pools or rivers to avoid desiccation.

Behavior

Primarily nocturnal or crepuscular, these semi-aquatic giants spend most of their lives submerged, ambushing prey like capybaras, caimans, birds, turtles, and fish by striking rapidly and coiling to suffocate victims. Home ranges expand in wet seasons for foraging and basking on low branches or banks, but shrink dramatically in dry times when they aestivate, burrowing into mud or congregating in residual pools. Solitary except during mating, they display defensive tactics like forming tight coils, hissing, or fleeing to water; juveniles are especially wary of predators.

Adaptations

Positioned high on their broad heads, eyes and nostrils allow surface breathing and vision while the body remains hidden underwater, perfecting stealthy aquatic hunts. Their olive-green scales, blotched with black and yellow, provide camouflage amid murky waters and dappled forest floors. Powerful, muscular builds enable constriction of outsized prey, sometimes 50% of their own mass, plus semi-arboreal climbing; they hold breath for 10+ minutes, tolerate low oxygen, and endure dry spells via metabolic slowdown and mud aestivation.

Overview

King cobras are the longest of all venomous snakes. As they face a variety of threats stemming from human activities, these snakes are vulnerable to extinction. The average life span of a King Cobra in the wild is 20 years. Its size is 13 feet and its weight is up to 20 pounds. Fortunately, King cobras are shy and will avoid humans whenever possible. It will also flare out its iconic hood and emit a hiss that sounds almost like a growling dog.

Habitat

King cobras live mainly in the rain forests and plains of India, southern China, and Southeast Asia, and their coloring can vary greatly from region to region. They are comfortable in a variety of habitats, including forests, bamboo thickets, mangrove swamps, high-altitude grasslands, and in rivers.

Behavior

True to their genus name which means snake-eater in Greek, the serpents feed mainly on other snakes, venomous and nonvenomous. Some have even been documented eating other king cobras. They will also eat lizards, eggs, and small mammals. King cobras are known to fight other males to win the attention of females during breeding season. They are the only snakes in the world that build nests for their eggs, which they guard ferociously until the hatchlings emerge.

Adaptations

When threatened, king cobras extend ribs in their neck to create a hood, raising up to one-third of their body, potentially human eye-level to ward off threats. They have specialized hollow fangs that deliver massive amounts of neurotoxic venom in a single bite.

Overview

The Gaboon viper (Bitis gabonica) is one of the largest and heaviest venomous snakes in the world, reaching lengths of up to 6 feet (1.8 meters) and weighing over 20 pounds. It is known for its extremely broad head, short but thick body, and highly patterned skin featuring geometric browns, purples, and creams that resemble fallen leaves. Despite being venomous, it is generally slow-moving and relies heavily on camouflage rather than aggression.

Habitat

Gaboon vipers are native to the rainforests and woodlands of sub-Saharan Africa, including countries such as Cameroon, Gabon, Nigeria, and the Democratic Republic of Congo. They prefer dense, humid forest floors where leaf litter allows them to blend in completely. They are most commonly found resting motionless in shaded areas near forest paths or burrows, where prey movement is frequent.

Behavior

Gaboon vipers are ambush predators that remain completely still for long periods, striking only when prey comes very close. Their diet includes small mammals, birds, and amphibians. They are generally calm and non-aggressive unless provoked, but their strike is extremely fast and powerful. They are primarily nocturnal, hunting at night when prey is active.

Adaptations

Their leaf-like camouflage is one of the most effective in the snake world, allowing them to become nearly invisible on the forest floor. They possess the longest fangs of any venomous snake (up to 2 inches), enabling deep venom delivery. Their venom is hemotoxic, breaking down blood cells and tissues to immobilize prey quickly.
