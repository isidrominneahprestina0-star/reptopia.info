Overview

Common chameleons (Chamaeleo chamaeleon), also known as Mediterranean chameleons, are small arboreal lizards about 17-25 inches long, famous for color-changing skin used in camouflage and communication rather than solely blending in. Native to warm regions, they are insectivores with independent eyes and a ballistic tongue, slowly navigating branches in deliberate movements.

Habitat

Common chameleons inhabit Mediterranean shrublands, savannas, riparian zones, forests, grasslands, pine forests, olive groves, orchards, and rural gardens from sea level to 800 m elevation, spanning southern Europe (Spain, Portugal, Greece, Cyprus), North Africa, and the Middle East. They prefer areas with trees or shrubs like carob for perching, avoiding extreme deserts but tolerating dry, coastal vegetation.

Behavior

Diurnal and arboreal, they perch motionless on branches for hours, slowly stalking insects before projecting their tongue to capture prey up to twice body length away. Territorial males change to brighter colors during displays or fights, puff up, gape, or hiss when threatened; females or duller. Solitary outside breeding, they brumate in cooler months, flick tongues to sense the environment, and rarely descend to ground.

Adaptations

Zygodactyl feet with fused toes grip branches securely for climbing; prehensile tail acts as a fifth limb for balance. Turreted eyes rotate 360 degrees independently for wide surveillance; chameleon skin with iridophores shifts hues for camouflage, thermoregulation, and signaling mood/aggression. Long, sticky tongue with hyoid bone launches at high speed (up to 60 mph); hooded head collects dew; slow gait minimizes detection by predators.

Overview

Reaching up to 10 feet in length and more than 300 pounds, Komodo dragons are the heaviest lizards on Earth. They have long, flat heads with rounded snouts, scaly skin, bowed legs, and huge, muscular tails.

Habitat

Komodo dragons have thrived in the harsh climate of Indonesia's Lesser Sunda Islands for millions of years. They prefer the islands’ tropical forests but can be found across the islands. Though these athletic reptiles can walk up to seven miles per day, they prefer to stay close to home, rarely venturing far from the valleys in which they hatched.

Behavior

Komodo Dragons are the main predator on the islands, they have poor eyesight and hearing but have a keen sense of smell. Komodos smell by flicking their forked tongue like a snake to pick up odors in the air. Scent-laden particles are carried to the Jacobson’s organ in the roof of their mouth. They can find injured or dead animals up to 5 miles away and can distinguish the scent of pregnant and non-pregnant prey.

Adaptations

Unlike bacteria-heavy saliva once thought to be their primary weapon, Komodo dragons use venom glands to secrete toxins that cause shock, severe hypotension, and prevent blood clotting, allowing them to kill large prey. They have flexible skulls and jaws, allowing them to swallow large, whole chunks of meat. Their stomach acid is strong enough to digest bones, hooves, and hides.

Overview

The marine iguana (Amblyrhynchus cristatus) is the world’s only sea-foraging lizard, found exclusively in the Galápagos Islands. They vary in size depending on the island but can reach up to 4 feet in length. Their dark, almost black coloration helps them absorb heat quickly after swimming in cold ocean waters.

Habitat

Marine iguanas inhabit the rocky coastlines of the Galápagos Islands in Ecuador. They spend most of their lives in intertidal zones, basking on volcanic rocks and diving into cold ocean waters to feed. Each island population shows slight variations in size and coloration depending on environmental conditions and food availability.

Behavior

Marine iguanas are social baskers, often seen grouped together on warm rocks to conserve heat. They dive into the ocean to feed primarily on algae, using strong jaws to scrape it off rocks. After feeding, they must warm up under the sun due to their drop in body temperature from cold-water diving. They are generally slow on land but agile swimmers in water.

Adaptations

They possess flattened tails and strong claws for swimming and gripping rocks in strong coastal currents. Specialized nasal glands help them expel excess salt by sneezing salt crystals from their bodies after feeding in seawater. Their dark coloration allows rapid heat absorption, and their ability to slow heart rate underwater helps conserve oxygen during dives.
